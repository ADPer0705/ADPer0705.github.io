### **The “Context Theory” of Relationships**

![[unnamed.jpg]]

### Why being “nice” or “good-looking” isn’t enough to make it work.

I spend a lot of time observing people. Being in college, you see the whole spectrum of relationships: the couples that have been together since high school, the new flings, and, most commonly, the people confused about why they are still single.

I constantly hear questions like: _“Why wouldn’t she like me?”_ or _“Why doesn’t he notice me?”_

The answer usually isn’t about looks, money, or luck. It’s about something I call The Context.

### The Missing Variable

When we think about our existing relationships — parents, best friends, roommates — we take them for granted. But if you look closely, every single one of those bonds has a “Base.”

- Parents: The base is biology, care, and years of shared history.
- Friends: The base might be a shared hobby (guitar lessons), a shared struggle (studying for finals), or a shared location (roommates).

This base is what I call a “Context” — a specific reason why you are in each other’s orbit. It’s the glue.

The mistake people make in romantic pursuits is trying to form a bond _without_ the glue. They think, _“I am handsome, she is pretty, therefore we should be together.”_

It’s not always about looks, either. You see people saying, _“We are classmates, we play the same sports, but we’re just not able to connect.”_ But that’s not how human beings work. You cannot build a house without a foundation, and you cannot build a relationship without Context. In many cases, the potential for context is there, but people fail to realize they need to actively build on it.

### The Two-Way Street (The Hard Truth)

Here is the part people miss, especially when they are crushing on someone: Every relationship is a transaction of value.

I don’t mean money. I mean emotional or social value.

- Your parents love you, but they get a sense of purpose and family in return.
- Your friend hangs out with you because you’re funny, or you play the same sport, or you help them with assignments.

There is always a force pulling from _both_ ends.

When you ask, _“Why doesn’t she like me?”_, the harsh answer is often: Because she has no reason to. You haven’t established a “pull” for her side of the equation yet. You might be the nicest guy in the room, but if you don’t share a context with her, you are just a background character in her life.

### How to “Build” the Context

So, you like someone. You have the “driving force” on your end. How do you get them to feel it too?

You have to look at the Context. This leaves you with two scenarios.

#### Scenario A: The Common Ground Exists

First, observe. Stop thinking about _your_ feelings and look at _their_ life. What do they do? What do they love?

- Do you share a class?
- Do you both play tennis?
- Are you both obsessed with the same weird genre of music?

If that overlap exists, build on it. That is your entry point.

- If it’s sports, challenge them to a match or invite them to watch one.
- If it’s food, ask for a recommendation or invite them to try a new spot.

_Note: This doesn’t have to be a “date” immediately._ It’s just utilizing the context to get on their radar.

#### Scenario B: The Cold Start (No Connection)

This is harder. If you have absolutely nothing in common — no classes, no mutual friends, no hobbies — you are fighting an uphill battle.

If you are motivated enough to pursue it, you have to manufacture the context. You cannot just stare at them and hope for telepathy. You have to create a shared experience.

- Go talk to them.
- Join the club they are in.
- Find an excuse to be in the same environment.

### The “Natural” Exception (What are they doing differently?)

I know what you’re thinking. _“But some people seem to figure this out with virtually no effort. What the hell are they doing differently?”_

In most cases, their “Context” is built-in. Maybe they have a vibe that fits perfectly with the crowd, or they are naturally charismatic conversation starters, or they have high social status. In their case, that status _is_ the pull. It acts as the basis for the interaction.

For them, the bridge is already built. For the rest of us? We have to build it ourselves.

### The Bottom Line

Romance isn’t just about “vibes.” It’s about having a place where your lives intersect.

If you want the relationship, don’t just focus on the person. Focus on the Context. Find the common ground, and if it’s not there, grab a shovel and build it.
